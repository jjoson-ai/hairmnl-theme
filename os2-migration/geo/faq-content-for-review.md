# HairMNL — FAQ Content for GEO / FAQPage Schema

**Purpose.** Draft FAQ question–answer sets for HairMNL's highest-value pages, to be added as `FAQPage` structured data (and a visible Q&A accordion). FAQPage is the schema type most cited by AI answer engines (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews), so adding it to lucrative product, collection, and guide pages improves the chance HairMNL is surfaced — and cited — when shoppers ask AI for recommendations.

**How these get applied.** Each page gets a `section-faq` section (theme's existing stock section) with one block per Q&A: `block.settings.title` = question, `block.settings.content` = answer. That section renders the visible accordion **and** emits the `FAQPage` JSON-LD automatically — no new code.

**Review notes.**
- Answers are intentionally evergreen (no prices — those live in the product's Offer schema and change often).
- Product/ingredient claims are grounded in current HairMNL product copy; please confirm any medical-adjacent phrasing (e.g. "seborrheic dermatitis") still matches brand/legal preference before publishing.
- Selection is data-driven: top revenue products + lucrative concern/brand collections + highest-traffic buying-guide & comparison articles that lack FAQ schema.
- Suggested scope: ~24 pages (6 products · 6 collections · 12 guides). Add or cut per your call.

## 15. Keratin Treatment vs Rebond — What's the Difference?
`/blogs/hair-education/keratin-treatment-vs-rebond-what-s-the-difference`

**Q: What's the difference between a keratin treatment and rebonding?**
A: Rebonding permanently restructures the hair to make it flat and pin-straight. A keratin treatment temporarily coats and fills the hair with protein to cut frizz while keeping your hair's natural body and movement.

**Q: Which lasts longer, keratin or rebond?**
A: Rebonding is permanent on the treated hair (new growth comes in natural), while a keratin treatment gradually washes out over a couple of months.

**Q: Which is less damaging?**
A: A keratin treatment is generally gentler because it doesn't permanently break the hair's internal bonds the way rebonding does. The right choice depends on your hair, so a professional consultation is recommended.

## 18. The Do's & Don'ts of Rebonded Hair in Summer
`/blogs/hair-education/the-do-s-and-don-ts-of-rebonded-hair-in-summertime`

**Q: How do I protect rebonded hair in the summer?**
A: Shield it from UV, sweat, humidity, and chlorine — wear a hat or use UV-protective products, rinse after swimming or heavy sweating, and keep hair moisturized to prevent dryness and reversion.

**Q: Can humidity ruin rebonded hair?**
A: High humidity and scalp sweat can trigger reversion (the return of frizz and waves) and dryness, so consistent protective care is key to keeping that sleek, straight finish.

**Q: What should I avoid with rebonded hair in summer?**
A: Avoid unprotected sun exposure, chlorinated pools without rinsing, and skipping conditioning — these lead to snapping, severe dryness, and faster reversion.

## 21. Low Porosity Hair — What It Is and What to Do
`/blogs/hair-education/low-porosity-hair-what-is-it-and-what-should-you-do-about-it`

**Q: How do I know if I have low porosity hair?**
A: Telltale signs: water takes a while to fully soak your hair, and products tend to sit on top rather than absorb — meaning the cuticle is tightly closed and resists moisture.

**Q: How should I care for low porosity hair?**
A: Use lightweight, easily-absorbed products, apply them to damp hair, and use gentle heat (like a warm towel) to help the cuticle open and let moisture in. Avoid heavy products that build up.

**Q: What products are best for low porosity hair?**
A: Lightweight leave-ins and humectant-based formulas absorb best — heavy butters and oils tend to sit on the surface and cause buildup.

## 22. Salon Hair Color vs Box Dye
`/blogs/hair-education/salon-hair-color-vs-box-dye-which-one-is-best-for-you`

**Q: Is salon color better than box dye?**
A: Salon color is customized to your hair by a professional for more even, predictable results and less risk of damage. Box dye is cheaper and convenient but one-size-fits-all, with a higher chance of uneven color or damage.

**Q: When should I choose box dye?**
A: Box dye can work for simple, all-over color close to your natural shade on a tight budget. For lightening, color correction, or big changes, a salon is the safer choice.

**Q: Can box dye damage my hair?**
A: It can — especially with lightening or repeated use — because the formula isn't tailored to your hair. Bond and moisture treatments help repair and protect color-treated hair.

## 23. Rinse-Out vs Leave-In Conditioner
`/blogs/hair-education/rinse-out-conditioner-vs-leave-in-conditioner-what-s-the-difference`

**Q: What's the difference between rinse-out and leave-in conditioner?**
A: A rinse-out (traditional) conditioner is applied after shampooing and washed out to soften and detangle. A leave-in stays in to give lasting moisture, detangling, and protection through the day.

**Q: Can I use both a rinse-out and a leave-in conditioner?**
A: Yes — many routines use a rinse-out conditioner in the shower for nourishment, then a leave-in afterward for extra moisture, frizz control, and protection.

**Q: Which conditioner is best for dry or damaged hair?**
A: Dry, damaged, or frizzy hair often benefits from both — a rich rinse-out plus a leave-in — while fine hair may prefer a lightweight leave-in to avoid weigh-down.

## 24. Hair Breakage — How to Fix Different Types of Damaged Hair
`/blogs/hair-education/hair-breakage-how-to-fix-different-types-of-damaged-hair`

**Q: What causes hair breakage?**
A: Common causes are heat styling, chemical treatments (color, bleach, rebond), tight hairstyles, and an improper care routine — all of which weaken the hair and make it brittle.

**Q: How do I fix damaged, breaking hair?**
A: Cut back on heat and tension, deep-condition regularly, and use bond-repair treatments like K18 or Olaplex to rebuild strength from within, alongside a gentle, sulfate-free routine.

**Q: Can broken hair be repaired or only cut off?**
A: Split ends ultimately need trimming, but bond-repair and protein/moisture treatments can reconnect internal bonds and prevent further breakage, making existing hair stronger and smoother.

HairMNL theme platform: readiness for the next major version

Our online store runs on a theme platform that is two major versions behind the current release. This brief explains why we should prepare for a future upgrade now — without committing to the upgrade itself yet — and what the choice costs either way.

02 / 12
Situation

Where we are today

HairMNL runs on Pipeline 6.1.3 — a theme platform released by Groupthought in 2022. The current Pipeline release is version 8.1.1, shipped in December 2025. We are two major versions behind.

What works

  • The site sells. Checkout, payments, and the order pipeline are healthy.
  • Performance is acceptable — we have invested heavily in tuning what the platform allows.
  • The theme vendor still maintains our version. There is no immediate end-of-life notice.

What is widening

  • New Shopify admin features (drag-and-drop section editing, AI section copy, theme blocks) are increasingly gated to modern themes.
  • Apps that we depend on for marketing, support, and recommendations are gradually shifting to a newer installation method that the older platform does not support natively.
  • Our customizations — the things that make the site uniquely ours — are written in idioms that newer developers no longer learn first.

The headline: Nothing is broken today. Everything we’re raising in this brief is about getting ready, not reacting.

03 / 12
Risk register

What could go wrong if we do nothing

Five risks, ranked by probability and impact. None of them are emergencies today. All of them get worse over a 12–24 month horizon.

HighApp-update breakage

Apps such as Klaviyo (email), Reamaze (chat), LimeSpot (recommendations), and Elevar (tracking) install into our theme using an older method. As vendors update their apps, they are gradually retiring that method in favor of a newer one that our platform does not support natively. When that happens, the integration can fail silently — we have already seen this once with the live chat widget disappearing for roughly ten days before anyone noticed.

HighSite speed has hit its ceiling

The prep-phase sandbox install of the next major theme version confirmed this is real and measurable: main-thread blocking time drops by 87–93% (the metric most tied to perceived responsiveness on phones) and layout shift goes to zero on a clean modern theme. Re-installing our apps would shrink those gains but the architectural wins on responsiveness and layout-stability survive. Page weight on the same store, same product catalog, drops by roughly half before apps and ~20% after. The exact field-data improvement (real customer experience) takes 4–8 weeks of real-user data to confirm after publishing, so lab gains do not directly translate — but the underlying architecture clearly outperforms ours.

MediumMerchandiser productivity

Each Shopify release adds features that only work on modern themes — section group editing, drag-and-drop everywhere, AI-assisted copy, and a forthcoming theme-blocks system. Our merchandising team is increasingly looking at “available in newer themes” banners in the editor.

Low probability, big impactVendor end-of-life

Pipeline 6 is in its fifth year. Groupthought still patches it, but every major theme eventually moves to maintenance-only and then to end-of-life. We have no announcement and probably months of warning if one comes, but the day will come.

Low velocity, compoundingTalent and maintenance cost

The platform idioms we depend on are legacy for any Shopify developer hired since 2023. Each new helper has a longer ramp-up before they can safely touch the codebase. This is slow-moving, but it compounds.

04 / 12
Boundaries

What is not a risk

It is worth being precise about what is — and isn’t — on the line, so that the case for action stays honest.

  • Checkout. Shopify migrated all stores to the new checkout in August 2025. That work is done; our theme version does not affect it.
  • The Shopify API and storefront API. These work independently of the theme. Headless integrations, mobile apps, and reporting tools are not affected by our theme version.
  • Google search ranking. Google penalizes slow sites, not old themes. Our SEO is a function of site speed and content quality, both of which we can continue to improve on the current platform — up to the ceiling described above.
  • Customer-visible behavior today. No customer is being turned away by our theme version. This brief is about getting ahead of a future failure mode, not patching a current one.

If anyone asks “why now?”, the answer is readiness, not repair.

05 / 12
Recommendation

The choice we are making

We are not proposing to migrate the theme right now. We are proposing to prepare for a migration, so that if and when one becomes necessary we can move quickly and from a position of knowledge rather than scramble.

Two postures, two costs

PostureWhat it doesCostLive-site risk
Wait-and-see (today’s default) Continue tuning the current platform. React to problems as they appear. None up front; high during eventual scramble. Vendor- and timeline-imposed.
Prepare-now (this proposal) Inventory what would need to change, pick a target platform, write the playbook, watch for triggers. About 1.5–2 weeks of work. None — no changes to the live site.
Migrate-now Port the entire site to a modern theme platform. About 3–6 weeks of work; tighter after the prep phase. Significant — full cutover.

The middle posture buys cheap optionality. The numbers come into focus, the decision is made with calm air, and if a trigger fires we are ready inside two weeks rather than eight.

06 / 12
Decision rules

The five triggers

We commit to a migration if any two of the following fire. These are the conditions we will be watching for over the next 12 months.

  1. A primary app breaks. Klaviyo, Reamaze, LimeSpot, or Elevar ships an update that removes support for our current installation method and live functionality degrades.
  2. Site speed plateaus. Mobile Core Web Vitals stop improving despite continued investment, and we have hit the architectural ceiling.
  3. Vendor end-of-life. Groupthought formally announces that Pipeline 6 is moving to maintenance-only or end-of-life.
  4. Onboarding cost crosses a threshold. A new developer taking over the site needs more than two weeks before they can ship safely.
  5. Merchandising blocker. The team needs a feature in the Shopify admin that is only available on modern themes and is genuinely blocking a planned campaign.

The trigger list is reviewed quarterly. If at any review the answer is “no triggers fired,” we keep the platform and revisit next quarter.

07 / 12
Cost — preparing

What preparing costs

Roughly 1.5–2 weeks of work for one operator (working with Claude), spread across three small phases. Nothing in this phase changes the live site.

PhaseWhat it producesEffortLive-site risk
1. Inventory App compatibility matrix; quantified list of our customizations; performance comparison vs. a sandboxed modern theme. 3–5 working days None — sandbox only.
2. Decision Pick the target platform (Pipeline 8, Shopify’s Dawn, or custom). Sign off on cost and timeline. Write the migration playbook. 2–3 working days None — documents only.
3. Watchlist Trigger definitions with measurable thresholds; vendor-changelog monitoring; an internal rule that stops us from adding new customizations that would have to be re-ported. ~1 working day None — process change only.

The output of this phase lives in the team’s documentation. It does not expire — the longer we wait to use it, the more refreshes it earns from quarterly trigger reviews.

08 / 12
Cost — migrating (only if triggered)

What a migration would cost

If a trigger fires and we decide to migrate, the work is real: roughly 3–6 weeks for one operator, gated on visual QA, an app-by-app re-integration pass, and a parallel-run period before cutover.

Where the time goes

  • Customization porting. The features that make our site different from a stock theme — bespoke product grids, performance optimizations, layout adjustments — have to be re-implemented in the new platform’s idioms.
  • App re-integration. Every app currently installed on the theme has to be reconnected. Some will gain native support and become easier; others may need workarounds.
  • Visual QA. Every template — product, collection, blog, cart, checkout, account — needs side-by-side comparison.
  • Parallel-run + cutover. The new theme runs side-by-side as a draft until we are confident, then we switch.

The 3–6-week range narrows substantially after the prep phase — the inventory tells us where the work concentrates, and the playbook tells us what order to do it in.

The theme vendor offers free major-version upgrades to existing customers. The cost above is engineering time, not licensing.

09 / 12
Why now, not later

Benefits of preparing now

Five things we get from spending two weeks on prep today.

  1. Cost certainty. “Three to six weeks” becomes a number with a confidence range we can plan budget against. Today it is a guess.
  2. Decision under calm. We pick the target platform on its merits, not under a vendor’s timeline or after a live integration has broken.
  3. Stop accruing debt. An internal rule prevents the next 12 months of theme work from adding customizations we will eventually have to re-port.
  4. Early-warning system. We watch the vendors most likely to deprecate the older installation method, so we hear about it from a release note — not from a broken customer experience.
  5. Playbook ready. If a trigger fires, the first day of the migration project starts with reading a document, not re-discovering the problem space.

11 / 12
Information value

What we know now — prep phase complete

A month ago we had guesses. Now we have numbers.

QuestionBefore prepWhat we know now
How much custom code would we need to port? “Some.” About 19,000 lines across 75 files. Roughly a fifth can be dropped, two-thirds need a straightforward port, and the remainder needs a rebuild.
How much speed would we gain from a modern theme? “Probably a lot.” Main-thread blocking time drops ~90%; layout shift goes to zero. Real-user impact takes 4–8 weeks post-publish to confirm.
Which apps would re-integrate cleanly? “Most of them, we think.” Of 30 apps audited: ~17 install cleanly on the modern theme via vendor-supported blocks; ~10 need verification or manual port; the rest are being replaced or removed anyway.
Which target platform is right for us? Three candidates, no committed answer. Pipeline 8.1.1 — same vendor as today, free upgrade, lowest porting cost, best feature parity. Dawn and a fully-custom headless build were both evaluated and ruled out for our scale.
What does a migration project actually look like? A vague 3–6 weeks. A 5-week sprint — 3.5–5.5 person-weeks of work — with a written, phase-by-phase playbook including rollback steps.

None of this commits us to migrating. It commits us to being ready — with budget, target, and playbook in hand — the moment a trigger fires.
